# Educational Path Public Source Lanes

Use official and dated sources first. Treat self-published biographies and social profiles as claims by the subject or publisher, not independently verified education facts.

## Official And Institutional Sources

- University, college, seminary, trade school, military academy, or training-provider biographies.
- Faculty/staff profile pages, archived versions, course catalogs, commencement programs, institutional repositories, alumni award pages, trustee pages, and public newsletters.
- Dissertation/thesis catalogs, library records, institutional repositories, ProQuest/Open Access records, ORCID, Crossref, PubMed author affiliations, Google Scholar pages as leads.
- Accreditation, licensure, certification, bar, medical board, professional board, or regulatory profiles when education/training is publicly listed.
- Public legislative, judicial, agency, military, campaign, or official government biographies.
- Court filings, sworn declarations, deposition transcripts, exhibits, CVs attached to expert reports, bankruptcy/SEC filings with executive bios.

## Archival And Historical Sources

- Public yearbooks, school newspapers, digitized archives, alumni magazines, commencement programs, society/program rosters, and institutional histories.
- Use archival student-era records carefully: they may mention private people, minors, addresses, or student organizations not relevant to the case.
- Capture page/volume/date and archive URL. Do not extract classmates or social circles unless central and source-supported.

## Professional And Publication Sources

- Academic articles and books: use author affiliations and bios only for what they state at publication time.
- Conference programs, speaker bios, grants, patents, and research-lab pages: useful for training/affiliation leads.
- Professional licensing databases: distinguish education, license status, specialty, discipline, and board certification.
- CVs/resumes: self-authored unless attached to a court filing, institutional profile, or official proceeding.

## Secondary Sources

- Reputable investigative reporting, local newspapers, obituaries, books, academic biographies, and documentary transcripts can corroborate or contextualize education facts.
- Campaign websites, personal websites, LinkedIn, social media, biography databases, speaker pages, and alumni directories are lead/self-report sources unless independently corroborated.
- Wikipedia, genealogy pages, cached snippets, and AI summaries are leads only; do not cite as evidence without underlying sources.

## Credential Dispute Sources

- Compare the original claim, later correction/retraction, institution response, court finding, licensing board record, or reputable reporting.
- Preserve exact wording. Do not accuse fabrication unless a source uses that label.
- Track "degree awarded," "attended," "completed coursework," "certificate," "honorary degree," "training program," and "license/certification" separately.

## Source Grading Defaults

- `A`: official institution record, government bio/filing, court record, licensing board record, public institutional archive, dissertation catalog.
- `B`: reputable reporting, documented biography/book, academic source, verified obituary, institutional publication not directly from the subject.
- `C`: personal/campaign/company bio, CV/resume, speaker bio, social profile, alumni page, memoir/interview.
- `D`: uncited database, genealogy page, forum, social-media repost, yearbook lead with unclear identity match.
- `X`: AI summary, unverifiable claim, non-public/private student record, leaked material without permission, fabricated/provenance-free content.
